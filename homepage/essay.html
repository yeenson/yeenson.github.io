This is my essay

Fort Hamilton Park

Long before you can even see the park, you know it is there. Laughter and screams that surround the park compel people towards its entrance, and they go because they are tired of silence. Had it been built anywhere else, the basketball courts in Fort Hamilton Park would stay empty on weekends, cracks would not form on the tennis courts, and the track would have never gotten its signature tire marks. But because there are no front lawns in my neighborhood and the driveways are reserved for cars, Fort Hamilton is the only place of community we have.

The community at Fort Hamilton consists of three groups: kids, families, and old people. Throughout the year, kids fill the basketball courts with pickup basketball games, whether it be to make new friends or strengthen bonds between existing ones. It does not matter who you are or even how you got to Fort Hamilton in the first place. As long as you are willing to open yourself up to the other kids, they will take you in. On the contrary are the families at Fort Hamilton. Parents who have finally gotten some free time off of work and just want to spend it with their kids, and kids who want nothing to do with their parents. It is these parents that just want the best childhood for their kids, and their only way to show them love is to bring them to Fort Hamilton. Finally, are the old people. At first notice, you can not help but curse them for the little things, like leisurely walking in front of your run on the track or taking up the tennis courts for games of pickleball. But you soon come to realize that they are no different than you are. Their time in the park is not just about physical health; it is about reclaiming their sense of youth, joy, and vitality. It is a chance for them to feel the way they once did. No matter which group they fit in, the people in my neighborhood depend on Fort Hamilton. They depend on it to keep them company, spend time with their loved ones, and feel a sense of belonging, and almost magically, Fort Hamilton always finds a way to fulfill that dependence.

Ever since I moved here, Fort Hamilton Park has always held a special place in my heart. I do not remember the first time I heard the laughter and screams from my bedroom window or the first time I opened the gate to the park, but I have many distinct memories of times when Fort Hamilton felt like a second home. I remember waking up tired on weekends and being dragged out of bed by my dad. I remember he insisted we play tennis early in the morning so he could train me to improve. No matter how well I played or how frustrated I got, he did not care. Looking back, I realize my dad just wanted to spend time with me, and Fort Hamilton gave him a chance to do so. On the contrary, I used to visit the park for different reasons. I remember times when the constant reminders of schoolwork or an upcoming test fogged up my mind to the point where I could not think straight anymore; when time started to speed up and all I needed was a place to stop and slow things down. That was when I would ride my bike down the block to Fort Hamilton. The repetitive rhythm of circling the track allowed worries to fade, and no matter how loud it was, the stress melted away like clockwork. In those moments, everything else was irrelevant. In those moments, it was just me, the track, and the freedom to clear my mind and find peace. In those moments, the park always had a way of telling you that everything would be fine.

In the end, know that Fort Hamilton is not just a very popular park but a place that holds the roots of strong relationships and special memories. The significance of this park extends far beyond its physical attributes. It has become a space of both social interactions and individual reflection as an escape from the overwhelming daily life, and this duality of Fort Hamilton exemplifies its importance in my neighborhood. The nature of Fort Hamilton Park as compared to the rest of my neighborhood is undoubtedly unusual, but it is this nature that binds my neighborhood together.
